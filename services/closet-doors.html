Closet Doors

Custom Closet Doors

Most closet doors are an afterthought — hollow panel assemblies that visually interrupt a well-designed room. A custom solid wood closet door ties the room together rather than standing apart from it.

Get a Free Estimate
What We Build

Closet Doors That Belong in the Room

Bifold configurations are the most common request for reach-in closets — they maximize access to the full opening width without requiring swing clearance into the room. We build bifold sets with the same panel profiles and wood species used on the other interior doors in the home, so the closet doesn't announce itself as a different product. Hardware is specified for smooth folding travel and positive closure, not just the cheapest pivot that fits.

Bypass sliding configurations are appropriate for wider openings — typically four feet or more — where bifolds would create awkward panel proportions, or where swing clearance is tight on both sides. We build bypass slabs in matched pairs so the face profiles align when one panel overlaps the other. The result looks intentional rather than like two independent doors that happen to be on the same track.

Barn-style hardware for closets is a practical solution when swing clearance doesn't exist and the wall space adjacent to the opening allows the door to travel fully open. We build these the same way we build standard barn doors — solid construction, correctly rated hardware — and keep the profile consistent with the rest of the room's woodwork so it doesn't feel like a stylistic detour.

For walk-in closets, the entry is a real door — hinged, with a proper frame, hardware, and reveal. We treat walk-in entries as part of the interior door package rather than a separate category, which means the profile, species, and finish match exactly. Primary bedroom walk-in closets in particular benefit from a door that reads as an extension of the room rather than a utility opening.

Why Custom Wood

What Makes Custom Closet Doors Worth It

  • — Matches your existing interior doors and trim exactly. Stock closet doors come in a limited set of profiles. If your interior doors have a specific panel shape, sticking profile, or species, stock options won't match. Custom doors are built to spec — same profile, same species, same finish.
  • — Solid wood won't warp or rack over time. Hollow panel bifolds are notorious for racking — the panels twist slightly and the hardware alignment degrades over years of use. Solid wood construction with proper joinery resists racking and stays true to the frame it was built for.
  • — Hardware specified for smooth operation under frequent use. Closet doors take more cycles per day than any other door in the house. We specify hardware designed for that load — pivots, rollers, and guides rated for durability, not just initial installation.
How It Works

The Build Process

From consultation to installed closet doors, here's how every project unfolds.

01

Consultation

We visit your home, measure every closet opening, and discuss configuration options — bifold, bypass, barn, or hinged — based on the available space and your style goals.

02

Design

We match the panel profile and wood species to your existing interior doors, confirm dimensions, and finalize the hardware package. You'll see exactly what we're building before any lumber is cut.

03

Build

Your doors are built from raw lumber in our shop. Bifold panels are built in matched pairs so the reveal is consistent across the full opening. Hardware attachment points are reinforced during the build.

04

Finish

Stain, paint, or clear-coat applied in multiple coats with sanding between each. Finish is matched to your existing interior doors — same sheen level, same tone, same application process.

05

Install

We hang every door, adjust hardware, and verify that every panel travels and closes correctly. Bifold alignment and bypass overlap are set precisely before we consider the job done.
